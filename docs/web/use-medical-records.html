Use Case: Medical Records

The Medical Records Problem

Much has been hoped for in the field of secure, portable medical records. Electronic Health Records (EHR) systems promise better care coordination and reduced medical errors, but they face a fundamental challenge:

Who can you trust to securely keep your medical record?

Current approaches have significant limitations:

  • Hospital-owned records: Your data is locked in each provider's system; moving care means starting fresh
  • EHR vendors: Centralized companies control access and can set terms unilaterally
  • Privacy concerns: Medical data breaches are common and devastating
  • Fragmentation: Your complete health history is scattered across multiple incompatible systems
  • Access barriers: Getting your own records can be difficult and slow
  • Patient powerlessness: You can't easily revoke a provider's access or control who sees what

The Sereus Solution: Patient-Owned Records

Under a Sereus topology, the answer to "who can you trust?" is simple: You.

How It Works

  1. Initial Setup: A patient initializes a blank medical record threadâ€”a shared database with initially only a single subscriber/keeper: themselves.
  2. Granting Provider Access: The patient shares a QR code with their doctor. The doctor's cadre scans it and suddenly has appropriate access to the database.
  3. Collaborative Records: The doctor begins storing relevant data (visit notes, prescriptions, test results) directly to the shared thread database.
  4. Multi-Provider Access: When the patient shares the database with a second provider, that provider gains access (if granted by the thread policies) to the existing medical record.
  5. Increased Resilience: As more providers join, data is sharding across a larger number of nodes, providing additional data security and availability.

Patient Control

The patient is always in charge of their own record:

  • Grant access: Add new providers by invitation/QR code
  • Revoke access: Terminate provider access at any time for any reason
  • Role-based permissions: Define what each provider can see and modify
  • Audit trail: See who accessed what data and when
  • Data ownership: The thread database belongs to you, not a hospital or vendor

Technical Implementation

A medical record thread might have a SQL schema including tables for:

  • Patient demographics and contact information
  • Visit history and clinical notes
  • Medications and prescriptions
  • Laboratory results and test reports
  • Imaging studies and reports
  • Immunization records
  • Allergies and adverse reactions
  • Care plans and treatment protocols

Role-based permissions ensure:

  • The patient can read everything
  • Providers can read relevant sections based on specialty
  • Only authorized providers can write to specific tables
  • Sensitive data (mental health, substance abuse) has additional access controls

Advantages Over Traditional EHR

  • Single source of truth: One comprehensive record, not fragments across systems
  • Immediate access: New providers see your complete history instantly upon invitation
  • Patient sovereignty: You control access, not the hospital or insurance company
  • Privacy by design: Data stored only on your cadre and authorized providers' cadres
  • Portability: Change providers without losing your records or starting over
  • Resilience: Data distributed across multiple nodes; survives individual device failures
  • Revocable access: Truly revoke a provider's access when switching care
  • Granular control: Share different data with different providers based on need
  • Audit transparency: Know exactly who accessed what and when

Security and Privacy

  • No central honeypot: Medical records aren't aggregated in one database to be breached
  • Encryption in transit: libp2p connections are encrypted
  • Consent-based access: Providers must be explicitly invited; can't just look you up
  • Role-based permissions: Fine-grained control over who sees what
  • Patient-controlled: You can remove access immediately if trust is broken
  • Distributed storage: Data sharded across trusted nodes reduces single-point vulnerabilities

Example Scenario

Sarah creates her medical record thread when she's young and healthy. Over the years:

  1. She invites her primary care physician who begins documenting her annual checkups
  2. When she needs a specialist, she shares access via QR code; the specialist sees relevant history
  3. She has a child and invites her OB/GYN; gives them access to relevant reproductive health data
  4. In an emergency, she shares temporary read access with ER staff from her phone
  5. Later, she revokes access to a provider she's no longer seeing
  6. Throughout, her complete medical history remains in one place, under her control

As more providers join, Sarah's record is automatically distributed across more nodes, improving availability without centralizing control.

Broader Implications

Patient-owned medical records on Sereus could enable:

  • Research participation: Patients selectively share de-identified data with researchers
  • Care coordination: All providers see a unified record, reducing duplicate tests
  • Family medical history: Share relevant genetic and family history across generations
  • Public health: Voluntary, consent-based reporting for epidemiology
  • Cross-border care: Medical records that work internationally
